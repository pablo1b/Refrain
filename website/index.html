An AI-native live-coding music IDE, built on Strudel

Compose the loop — by hand or by intention.

Refrain is a workspace where a Strudel score and a musical conversation are two views of one composition. Type TidalCycles-style patterns directly, or speak to the Maestro in plain musical language — crescendo, half-time the bass, reharmonise in Dorian — and watch the code change, audition it on the next downbeat, and accept it. Every AI action is a real, inspectable edit. Nothing is a black box.

01

Intent and code are two views of the same score.

Strudel made algorithmic music playable in a browser, but a gap remains. For a trained musician, the distance between what they hear in their head and the pattern syntax that produces it is wide. For a fluent live-coder mid-performance, hand-editing every nuance is slow when the room is waiting. Refrain closes the gap from both sides without taking the instrument away from either player.

The premise is simple and strict: the code is always the score. When you ask the Maestro to “make the hats swing,” it doesn’t render audio behind your back — it writes a diff against your actual pattern, shows you the lines it touched, auditions the change on the next cycle, and waits for your nod. You can read everything it does. You can edit it afterward. You can undo it in one keystroke.

Every AI action is a visible, reversible edit to code you own — trustworthy enough to use on stage, rigorous enough to compose with.

03

The Maestro answers in three shapes.

Not a chatbot bolted to an editor. The Maestro reads your project as a parsed Strudel tree, reasons about music, and answers in exactly one of three shapes — each inspectable, auditioned before it commits, and one keystroke from undo.

Shape 01

A diff

Edits to existing code, hunk by hunk. For changes: make the hats swing, darker pad, half-time the bass. You accept or reject each hunk; it auditions live first.

Shape 02

Variation lanes

New material as 2–4 forks to audition against the live mix. For generation: three ways into the drop. Solo each, tweak one, commit one.

Shape 03

An answer

Explanation or annotation — no code change. For learning: what does this line do, musically? Inline notes attach to the score, dismissable.

Directives — musical verbs as typed commands

A vague prompt invites a vague answer. A directive is a curated musical term with bounded, reproducible behaviour: the Maestro chooses the targets and the degree, but the shape of the transform is known. Type / to summon the palette.

Grounded, not guessing

Edits a parsed AST and diffs against real nodes — it can only change code that exists, and it cites the exact lines it touched.

Nothing reaches the speakers unvetted

Every proposal is parsed and dry-run-rendered before it can audition. A generation that errors stays in its lane.

Routed by task

A fast model handles directives; a strong model handles generation and theory; a local model covers offline gigs.

04

The Cycle — the signature instrument.

Strudel thinks in cycles, not bars-and-beats. Refrain makes the cycle a first-class object you can see, point at, and command. A radial reading of one cycle: each voice is a ring, each event a tick, a single playhead sweeping the present.

  • The clock everything quantises to. Edits land on the next cycle boundary; the sweep shows exactly how long until a staged change goes live.
  • A target, not just a readout. Spatial language — “the first beat,” “every off-beat,” “the back half” — resolves to concrete time-spans the Maestro can edit.
  • It scales to the room. In Performance mode the clock fills the screen and becomes the show — legible from the back of a venue.
05

Six principles

i.

The code is the score, always.

Every AI action produces visible, reversible code — never hidden state. If you can hear it, you can read it, and change it by hand.

ii.

Two hands, one instrument.

Typing and talking are equal inputs, interleaved freely. The Maestro proposes; it never seizes the cursor or stops you mid-phrase.

iii.

Nothing breaks the sound.

Edits are validated and staged, then auditioned at a cycle boundary before they touch the running mix. Panic is one key away.

iv.

Speak music, not prompts.

Commands are musical directives — crescendo, rubato, cantabile, stretto — with defined, bounded behaviour. Not vague chat that guesses.

v.

Everything branches & rewinds.

Time-travel the score, fork variations, A/B them against the live mix. Composition is a tree you explore, not a line you erase.

vi.

Local-first; your keys, your sound.

Audio runs offline on Web Audio. Bring your own model keys. Samples, patches and history live on your disk.

06

Built for three players at once

The tension between them — speed vs. rigour vs. literacy — is the design’s most productive constraint.

V

Vera — the live-coder

algoraves · improvisation

Performs from a dark stage. Thinks in cycles and energy curves. Will type fast, then talk fast when her hands can’t keep up with the room.

J

Jonas — new to code

conservatory pianist

Reads a score at sight, hears harmony in colour, writes code haltingly. Knows exactly what he wants and lacks only the syntax to say it.

M

Mara — the producer

studio · arrangement

Builds pieces over weeks across many files. Designs her own patches and sample banks. Cares about structure, history, and stems.
