Refrain
Design specification · v0.1 · June 2026

An AI-native live-coding music IDE, built on Strudel

Compose the loop — by hand or by intention.

Refrain is a desktop IDE where a Strudel score and a musical conversation are two views of one composition. Type TidalCycles-style patterns directly, or speak to the Maestro in plain musical language — crescendo, half-time the bass, reharmonise in Dorian — and watch the code change, audition it on the next downbeat, and accept it. Every AI action is a real, inspectable edit. Nothing is a black box.

Platform
Standalone desktop app (Tauri). Local-first. Reuses the real Strudel CodeMirror REPL.
Sound
Pure Strudel / Web Audio (superdough). Offline-capable. Your samples & patches on disk.
Intelligence
Bring-your-own-key. Anthropic · OpenAI · Google · local Ollama, routed by task.
01

Intent and code are two views of the same score.

Strudel made algorithmic music playable in a browser, but a gap remains. For a trained musician, the distance between what they hear in their head and the pattern syntax that produces it is wide. For a fluent live-coder mid-performance, hand-editing every nuance is slow when the room is waiting. Refrain closes the gap from both sides without taking the instrument away from either player.

The premise is simple and strict: the code is always the score. When you ask the Maestro to “make the hats swing,” it doesn’t render audio behind your back — it writes a diff against your actual pattern, shows you the lines it touched, auditions the change on the next cycle, and waits for your nod. You can read everything it does. You can edit it afterward. You can undo it in one keystroke. The AI is a second pair of hands on the same instrument, never a vending machine bolted to the side.

That single decision — every AI action is a visible, reversible edit to code you own — is what makes Refrain trustworthy enough to use on stage and rigorous enough to compose with.

02

Six principles

i.

The code is the score, always.

Every AI action produces visible, reversible code — never hidden state. If you can hear it, you can read it, and you can change it by hand.

ii.

Two hands, one instrument.

Typing and talking are equal inputs, interleaved freely. The Maestro proposes; it never seizes the cursor or stops you mid-phrase.

iii.

Nothing breaks the sound.

Edits are validated and staged, then auditioned at a cycle boundary before they touch the running mix. The transport never stutters. Panic is one key away.

iv.

Speak music, not prompts.

Commands are musical directives — crescendo, rubato, cantabile, stretto — with defined, bounded behaviour. Not vague chat that guesses.

v.

Everything branches & rewinds.

Time-travel the score, fork variations, A/B them against the live mix. Composition is a tree you explore, not a line you erase.

vi.

Local-first; your keys, your sound.

Audio runs offline on Web Audio. Bring your own model keys. Samples, patches and history live on your disk — a gig laptop owes nothing to the network.

03

Three players

Refrain optimises for three people at once. The tension between them — speed vs. rigour vs. literacy — is the design’s most productive constraint.

V

Vera — the live-coder

algoraves · improvisation

Performs from a dark stage. Thinks in cycles and energy curves. Will type fast, then talk fast when her hands can’t keep up with the room.

Needs

Cycle-quantised edits that never drop the beat · a panic / hush key · huge legible visuals · instant forks to gamble on.

J

Jonas — the musician, new to code

conservatory pianist

Reads a score at sight, hears harmony in colour, writes code haltingly. Knows exactly what he wants and lacks only the syntax to say it.

Needs

The notation bridge · musical directives in his own vocabulary · “explain this line as music” · theory operations that respect the key.

M

Mara — the producer / composer

studio · arrangement

Builds pieces over weeks across many files. Designs her own patches and sample banks. Cares about structure, revision history, and stems.

Needs

Multi-file projects · the patch designer & sample foundry · an arrangement timeline · provenance for every generated part.

04

Anatomy of the workspace

Four surfaces around one transport. The Score is the real Strudel editor — nothing about hand-coding changes. Everything else is arranged so the other two players (the Maestro and your ears) are always within a glance and a keystroke.

A · The Shelf
Project files, the live voice outline, patches & sample packs.
B · The Score
The real Strudel CodeMirror editor — active-event highlight, inline ⌘K.
C · The Maestro
Conversational pair. Every turn becomes a diff, a variation, or an answer.
D · The Stage
The Cycle clock, per-voice meters, solo/mute, CPU & headroom.

Themeable, not skinned

Dark is the default — it belongs on a stage and at 2 a.m. The light theme is the same layout for daylight studio work, with the colour language preserved: live stays a deep lime, the Maestro stays amber.

05

The Cycle — the signature instrument

Strudel thinks in cycles, not bars-and-beats. Refrain makes the cycle a first-class object you can see, point at, and command. The Cycle clock is a radial reading of one cycle: each voice is a ring, each event a tick, and a single playhead sweeps the present. It is the canonical answer to “what is happening right now,” and the target for any spatial instruction.

It is the clock everything quantises to

Edits land on the next cycle boundary. The sweep shows you exactly how long until a staged change goes live — so you can hit it on the phrase.

It is a target, not just a readout

Spatial language — “the first beat,” “every off-beat,” “the back half” — resolves to concrete time-spans the Maestro can edit precisely.

It scales to the room

In Performance mode the clock fills the screen and becomes the show — legible from the back of a venue, honest about what each voice is doing.

06

The Maestro

The Maestro is not a chatbot bolted to an editor. It reads your project as a parsed Strudel tree, reasons about music, and answers in exactly one of three shapes — each one inspectable, auditioned before it commits, and one keystroke from undo.

SHAPE 01

A diff

Edits to existing code, hunk by hunk. Used for changes: make the hats swing, darker pad, half-time the bass. You accept or reject each hunk; it auditions live first.

SHAPE 02

Variation lanes

New material, offered as 2–4 forks to audition against the live mix. Used for generation: three ways into the drop. Solo each, tweak one, commit one.

SHAPE 03

An answer

Explanation or annotation — no code change. Used for learning: what does this line do, musically? Inline notes attach to the score, dismissable.

Directives — musical verbs as typed commands

A vague prompt invites a vague answer. A directive is a curated musical term with bounded, reproducible behaviour. The Maestro chooses the targets and the degree; the shape of the transform is known — so directives are predictable, fast, and cheap enough to route to a small or local model. Type / in the Maestro to summon the palette.

DYNAMICS
crescendoramps .gain over n cycles— grow into the drop
diminuendogain ↓ + opens space— pull back for a breakdown
sforzandoaccent one event hard— punch the downbeat
AGOGICS
rubatohumanises timing (nudge)— loosen the grid
accel. / rit.cps ramps up / down— a controlled gear-change
strettooverlaps entries tighter— pile the canon up
ARTICULATION
staccatoshortens .clip / release— make it bite
legatolengthens + glides— smooth the line
marcatoper-event accent map— march it
CHARACTER
cantabilesmooth gain + voice-lead— make it sing
con fuocodrive, dist, faster LFO— with fire
misteriosoreverb, lpf, sparse— make it haunt

Directive packs are extensible — a user can author their own verb (“molto cantabile”) and bind it to a transform. The vocabulary is yours.

Grounded, not guessing

The Maestro edits a parsed AST and diffs against real nodes — it can only change code that exists, and it cites the exact lines it touched. No silent rewrites of your whole file.

Nothing reaches the speakers unvetted

Every proposal is parsed and dry-run-rendered before it can audition. A generation that errors stays in its lane and never touches the live pattern. Undo is always one cycle back.

Routed by task

A fast/cheap model handles directives & completions; a strong model handles generation, arrangement and theory; a local model (Ollama) covers offline gigs. You map roles to models.

07

The composing surfaces

Eight focused surfaces, each opened in context from the workspace. They share the chrome, the colour grammar, and the rule that everything resolves to code you can read.

7.1 — SEMANTIC EDIT · INLINE DIFF

Edits arrive as a diff you accept hunk by hunk

⌘K on a selection, or a message to the Maestro, stages a diff directly in the Score. It auditions on the next cycle so you hear it before you keep it; accept all with ⏎, or toggle hunks individually.

7.2 — GENERATION · VARIATION LANES

New material arrives as lanes you audition against the mix

Generation never overwrites. The Maestro returns two to four forks; each solos into the running mix on click, shows its own cycle preview, and can be refined with a follow-up. Commit one into a named scene — the others stay parked in the variation tree.

7.3 — PATCH DESIGNER

Design a synth voice as a signal chain — or describe it

Open the Patch Designer on any sound. Tweak the chain by hand on the node graph, or tell the Maestro “warmer, more analog, longer tail.” Both edit the same thing: a named Strudel signal chain saved to the Shelf and referenced by name in your score. Preview plays on a held note while you turn knobs.

7.4 — SAMPLE FOUNDRY

Generate, find, and chop sound — land it as a playable bank

One surface, three sources: generate a sample neurally from a description and seed, search across your local packs by sound, or upload. Whatever you pick is auto-sliced into an n-map and dropped onto the Shelf with a one-line usage snippet inserted at your cursor.

7.5 — THEORY OPS & THE NOTATION BRIDGE

For the musician: read it as a score, command it as theory

Hover any pitched pattern to see it on a staff with a Roman-numeral harmonic readout. Edit on the staff and the code updates; edit the code and the staff follows. Theory operations — reharmonise, voice-lead, modulate, invert, add counterpoint — are constrained to the key and previewed on both staff and audio before they commit.

7.6 — ARRANGEMENT

Scenes on a timeline; the Maestro writes the transitions

For pieces that have a shape, lay named scenes on a timeline — intro, build, drop, break, outro. Each scene is a snapshot of which voices play and how. Ask for a transition between two scenes and the Maestro writes the automation (a filter sweep, a riser, a drop-out); rehearse it, then lock it. In Performance mode the same scenes become launch buttons.

7.7 — PERFORMANCE MODE

For the stage: the Cycle becomes the show

One keystroke clears the chrome. The Cycle clock fills the screen, scenes become big MIDI-mappable launch buttons, and every Maestro action is cycle-quantised so nothing lands off the beat. PANIC hushes all voices instantly without stopping the transport — the safest possible exit.

7.8 — PROVIDERS & ROUTING

Bring your own keys; route models by task

Refrain talks to whichever providers you hold keys for — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, or a local Ollama model for offline gigs. Keys live in the OS keychain. You then map roles to models, so cheap-and-fast handles directives while a stronger model handles generation and theory. A single local-only switch guarantees nothing leaves the machine.

08

Key flows

Eight paths through the tool. Two things recur in every one: an audition step before anything commits, and a cycle-quantised commit so the music never breaks.

A

Direct coding — the Strudel REPL, preserved

type a pattern→ ghost-completion offers next→ ⌘⏎ evaluate→ colored flash + hear it

Errors read as music, not stack traces: “$bass has no sound — add .s(…)”. The Maestro is entirely optional here.

B

Semantic edit — “make the hats swing”

⌘K or ask→ staged diff in Score→ auto-audition next cycle→ accept / reject per hunk→ commit on downbeat
C

Generate a section — variation lanes

prompt or drag reference→ 2–4 lanes returned→ solo each vs. mix→ refine the keeper→ commit to named scene
D

Patch design

open on a sound ↓ graph / knobs / describe ↓ preview on held note ↓ save to Shelf by name
E

Sample foundry

describe / search / upload ↓ render or pick ↓ auto-slice → n-map ↓ snippet inserted at cursor
F

Theory operation — reharmonise / modulate / voice-lead

select a pitched pattern→ choose op (key-constrained)→ preview on staff + audio→ cycle voicings ↻→ accept
G

Arrangement transition

lay scenes on timeline→ ask: transition build→drop→ Maestro writes automation→ rehearse it→ lock
H

Performance

enter Live mode→ map scenes/directives to MIDI→ improvise · all cycle-quantised→ PANIC = hush, clock safe
09

The composing model

Four commitments make Refrain feel like an instrument a trained musician can trust — not a slot machine.

The cycle is the unit

Every change — typed, spoken, or generated — quantises to a cycle or phrase boundary. You always know when it lands, and the music never stutters mid-thought.

Determinism where it matters

Directives are reproducible transforms. Generation is seeded — re-roll with the same seed for the same result, or nudge the seed to wander. Randomness is a choice, never an accident.

A bridge for notation literacy

Staff view, Roman-numeral harmony, and “explain this line as music” let a musician who reads fluently meet the code halfway — learning the syntax through sound they already understand.

Provenance — a musical changelog

Every scene and generated part remembers the prompt, directive, seed, and model that made it. The history is a tree you can walk, fork, and cite — revision control that speaks music.

The machine should be able to surprise you — but only on the beat, and only into code you can read tomorrow.

10

Design system & rationale

A colour language with meaning, not decoration

Three signal colours carry fixed meaning across every surface, so the eye learns them once. Code syntax sits in its own cool palette so it never competes.

Lime
live · transport · the cycle
Amber
the Maestro · AI authorship
Blue
selection · structure
Ink
stage surface (dark default)

Type — the concert programme meets the terminal

Aa
SPECTRAL
Display — headlines, pull-quotes, the score-programme voice.
Aa
HANKEN GROTESK
Body & UI — copy, labels, every interface surface.
Aa
JETBRAINS MONO
Code, transport, hex, all technical chrome.

Keyboard-first

Every action has a chord. ⌘K is the Maestro inline; ⏎ accepts; the transport keys never move. Hands stay home.

Accessible by construction

Meters carry numeric read-outs; colour is never the only signal; prefers-reduced-motion stills the sweep without losing the count.

Settling, not bouncing

Motion is physical and brief — a diff settles in, the sweep is metronomic. No springs, no confetti. The music is the delight.

Local-first

Audio is offline Web Audio. Files, samples, patches and history are on disk. Only LLM calls touch the network — and local-only mode stops even those.

11

Open questions & next

Decisions deliberately left open, and the obvious next moves once the core holds.

NEXT
MIDI & OSC out. Out of scope for v1 (pure Web Audio), but the Tauri/Rust shell exists precisely to add hardware MIDI and OSC-to-SuperDirt later without a browser sandbox fight.
EXPLORE
Live collaboration. Shared sessions with multiplayer cursors — two coders, one set. Conflict model and latency on the cycle clock are the open hard parts.
EXPLORE
Directive & patch marketplace. Shareable packs of custom musical verbs, patches and sample banks — a culture of vocabulary, not just presets.
OPEN
A tablet performance companion. The Cycle clock + scene launchers as a touch surface beside the laptop. Worth it, or a distraction from the keyboard-first core?
OPEN
Notation-bridge fidelity limits. Polyrhythm, microtonality and dense polyphony resist a clean staff. Where does the bridge gracefully say “this is better read as code”?
